# Chapter 1 — Numbers Before Writing

I should tell you upfront that I don't fully believe what this book is about to propose.

That isn't false modesty. I have a doctorate. I was trained to distrust elegant systems, to look for the seam where the theory splits from reality. And yet here I am, years into studying a system built around a deck of playing cards, and I still can't find the seam.

So I'll tell you what I know, which is less than it sounds. And I'll tell you what I've observed, which is more than it should be. You can decide what to do with the gap between those two things.

Let's start at the beginning — not the beginning of this system, but the beginning of numbers themselves. Because before we can talk about what a number *means*, we have to reckon with what a number *is*.

---

**Before the symbol, there was the hand.**

The oldest counting we know of isn't written. It's carved. A baboon's fibula, found in Swaziland, notched with 29 marks in groups. A wolf bone from Czechoslovakia with 55 notches arranged in sets of five. These objects are between 40,000 and 43,000 years old. Nobody taught their makers arithmetic. Nobody handed them a system.

They just needed to keep track of something — and their bodies already knew how.

This is the part I find self-evident in a way I can't justify: counting feels like *remembering*, not *inventing*. The hand has five fingers. Two hands make ten. A lunar cycle is roughly 29 days — close enough to a month that you could track it on a bone before you had a word for "month." These aren't cultural constructions. They're physical facts that kept showing up in the same places independently: Mesopotamia, Egypt, China, Mesoamerica. Separated by oceans and millennia. All arriving at the same conclusions.

You could say that's because humans all have the same number of fingers. That would be the skeptic's answer, and it's a reasonable one. I've given it myself.

But it doesn't explain everything.

---

**The leap that shouldn't have been possible.**

At some point — nobody knows exactly when or where — something shifted. A human being looked at three stones, three fingers, three days, and understood that the *threeness* was the same thing across all of them. That "three" wasn't a property of stones or fingers or days. It was a property of *sets*.

This is a staggering abstraction. Children don't grasp it automatically; developmental psychologists have documented the exact age at which the concept clicks, and it doesn't click for everyone at the same time. And yet our ancestors made this leap without formal education, without written language, without anyone to explain it to them.

The number had separated from the thing it counted. A symbol had been born.

I don't know what to make of that. The cognitive scientist in me says it was evolutionary pressure — better pattern recognition, better survival. The part of me writing this book says something else, something I'm less comfortable saying out loud: that maybe numbers were always there, and humans didn't invent them so much as *find* them.

Pythagoras said as much, 2,500 years ago. I'm not ready to sign his name to my thesis. But I'm not ready to dismiss him either.

---

**The same answers, from different directions.**

Around 3000 BCE, the Mesopotamians developed a positional number system based on 60. The Egyptians developed base-10. The Babylonians used base-12 for certain measurements. These systems emerged in different civilizations, with different languages, different gods, different economies.

And yet they weren't random. Base-10 follows the fingers. Base-12 follows the phalanges of four fingers counted by the thumb — a counting method still used in parts of the Middle East today. Base-60 is divisible by 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 12, 15, 20, and 30 — making it extraordinarily practical for fractions, for time, for astronomical calculation.

There are 60 seconds in a minute. 60 minutes in an hour. 360 degrees in a circle. We still use Babylonian math every time we check the clock.

The skeptic in me notes: practical systems survive because they work. People kept using base-60 because it was useful, not because it was sacred.

The other part of me notes: that these systems, independently derived, kept converging on the same numbers — 7, 12, 13, 52, 360 — is either a remarkable coincidence or evidence of something structural underneath. Something the numbers are pointing at whether we intend them to or not.

---

**What we can say with confidence.**

Numbers preceded writing by tens of thousands of years. The first written records we have — the Sumerian clay tablets, the Egyptian papyri — weren't poems or histories. They were accounting ledgers. Numbers came first.

More than that: every civilization that achieved complexity developed a numerical system. Not some of them. All of them. The number wasn't optional. It was the precondition for everything that followed — for architecture, for agriculture, for astronomy, for law.

That's not mysticism. That's archaeology.

What archaeology can't tell us is *why* the same numbers keep appearing across unconnected civilizations. Why 7 is the number of visible celestial bodies and also the number of days in a week and also the number of notes in a diatonic scale and also, as we'll see later, the number of planetary periods in the system at the center of this book.

Probably a coincidence.

I've been telling myself that for years.
